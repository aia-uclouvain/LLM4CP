name: Changepoint Detection Problem
description: The Changepoint Detection Problem is a classic problem in time series analysis and probabilistic programming. It involves identifying a point in a time series where the underlying distribution or behavior of the data changes. This change can be sudden or gradual, and the goal is to detect the point at which this change occurs. The problem has numerous applications in fields such as finance, climate science, and signal processing.
variables: $cp, mean1, mean2, mean\_diff1, mean\_diff2, z$ - These variables represent the decision variables in the problem. $cp$ is the changepoint, which is the point in the time series where the change occurs. $mean1$ and $mean2$ are the means of the data before and after the changepoint, respectively. $mean\_diff1$ and $mean\_diff2$ are the sum of absolute differences between the data points and the mean before and after the changepoint, respectively. $z$ is the total sum of absolute differences, which is the objective function to be minimized.
constraints: $mean1 = \sum_{i=1}^{len} (i < cp) \* data[i] / cp$ and $mean2 = \sum_{i=1}^{len} (i \geq cp) \* data[i] / (len - cp + 1)$ and $mean\_diff1 = \sum_{i=1}^{len} (i < cp) \* |data[i] - mean1|$ and $mean\_diff2 = \sum_{i=1}^{len} (i \geq cp) \* |data[i] - mean2|$ and $z = mean\_diff1 + mean\_diff2$ - These constraints define the relationships between the decision variables. The first two constraints calculate the means of the data before and after the changepoint. The next two constraints calculate the sum of absolute differences between the data points and the mean before and after the changepoint. The final constraint defines the objective function $z$ as the sum of $mean\_diff1$ and $mean\_diff2$.
objective: Minimize $z$ - The objective of the problem is to minimize the total sum of absolute differences $z$, which represents the total deviation of the data from the means before and after the changepoint. By minimizing $z$, we can identify the changepoint that best explains the change in the underlying distribution of the data.
