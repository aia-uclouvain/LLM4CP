The Hardest Logic Puzzle Ever, also known as the Knight, Knave, and Normal problem. 
This problem is about determining the nature of three individuals, A, B, and C, who can be either a knight (always tells the truth), a knave (always lies), or a normal (sometimes tells the truth, sometimes lies), based on their statements about each other.
The main decision variables are the types of A, B, and C, represented by the array `p`, where `p[1]`, `p[2]`, and `p[3]` correspond to the types of A, B, and C, respectively, with 1 representing a knight, 2 representing a normal, and 3 representing a knave.
The key constraints are that the statements made by each individual must be consistent with their type, and that the types of A, B, and C must be distinct. For example, if A says that A is normal, then A must be either a knight (telling the truth) or a normal (sometimes lying). If B says that A is not a knight, then B must be either a knight (telling the truth) or a normal (sometimes lying).
The objective of the problem is to find a consistent assignment of types to A, B, and C that satisfies all the constraints, with no specific minimization or maximization goal, as the problem is a satisfaction problem.
