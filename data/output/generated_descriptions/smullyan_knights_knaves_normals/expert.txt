name: Smullyan Knight, Knave, and Normal problems

description: The problem involves determining the types of three individuals, A, B, and C, who can be either knights (always tell the truth), knaves (always lie), or normals (sometimes tell the truth, sometimes lie). Each individual makes a statement about themselves or others, and the goal is to find the possible types of A, B, and C that satisfy all the given statements.

variables: $p = (p_1, p_2, p_3)$, where $p_i \in \{1, 2, 3\}$ represents the type of individual $i$, with $1$ denoting a knight, $2$ denoting a normal, and $3$ denoting a knave.

constraints: 
$\begin{aligned}
&says(p_1, p_1 = normal) \\
&says(p_2, p_1 = normal) \\
&says(p_3, not(p_3 = normal)) \\
&all\_different(p) \\
&says(p_1, p_2 = knight) \\
&says(p_2, not(p_1 = knight)) \\
&says(p_1, p_2 = knight) \\
&says(p_2, p_1 = knave) \\
&says(p_1, -p_1 < -p_2) \\
&says(p_2, not(-p_1 < -p_2)) \\
&says(p_1, -p_2 > -p_3) \\
&says(p_2, -p_3 > -p_1) \\
&says(p_3, p_{43\_C\_says\_B\_higher\_rank\_than\_A}) \\
&p_{43\_C\_says\_B\_higher\_rank\_than\_A} = (-p_2 > -p_1)
\end{aligned}$ 
These constraints represent the statements made by each individual and the relationships between their types, as well as the condition that all individuals must have different types.

objective: satisfy, meaning the goal is to find the possible assignments of types to A, B, and C that satisfy all the given constraints, without optimizing any specific value.
