Taxicab Problem
The Taxicab problem is about finding two sets of integers, each set consisting of three or four numbers, such that the sum of the cubes of the numbers in one set equals the sum of the cubes of the numbers in the other set.

The main decision variables in this problem are A, B, C, D, E, F, and G, which represent the integers in the two sets. The variables A, B, C, and D represent the numbers in the first set, while the variables E, F, and G represent the numbers in the second set.

The key constraints in this problem are: 
- The sum of the cubes of A, B, C, and D must be equal to the sum of the cubes of E, F, and G. 
- The numbers within each set must be in non-decreasing order (A ≤ B ≤ C ≤ D and E ≤ F ≤ G). 
- The smallest number in the first set must be less than or equal to the smallest number in the second set (A ≤ E).

The objective of the Taxicab problem is not to minimize or maximize a specific value, but rather to find a solution that satisfies the given constraints, i.e., to find two sets of integers whose cubes sum up to the same value. The problem is a classic example of a constraint satisfaction problem, where the goal is to find a feasible solution that meets all the constraints.
