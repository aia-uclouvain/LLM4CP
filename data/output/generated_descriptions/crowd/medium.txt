The Crowded Chessboard puzzle
The Crowded Chessboard puzzle is about placing different types of chess pieces (queens, bishops, knights, and rooks) on a chessboard in such a way that the total number of pieces is minimized while ensuring that no piece attacks another piece of the same type.

The main decision variables in this problem are represented by the 3D array `x`, where `x[i, j, k]` is a binary variable that indicates whether a piece of type `k` is placed on the square at position `(i, j)` on the board. The types of pieces are represented by the values 1 (queen), 2 (bishop), 3 (knight), and 4 (rook).

The key constraints in this problem can be described as follows: 
- Each type of piece must be placed a specific number of times on the board, as specified by the `N` array.
- Each square on the board can be occupied by at most one piece.
- No queen can attack another queen, which means that no two queens can be in the same row, column, or diagonal.
- No bishop can attack another bishop, which means that no two bishops can be in the same diagonal.
- No rook can attack another rook, which means that no two rooks can be in the same row or column.
- No knight can attack another knight, which means that no two knights can be in a position to jump over each other.

The objective of this problem is to minimize the total number of pieces placed on the board, which is represented by the variable `z`. The problem is solved using a constraint programming approach, where the constraints are defined using the MiniZinc language, and the solution is found using a search algorithm.
