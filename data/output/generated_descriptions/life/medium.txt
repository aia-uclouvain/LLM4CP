Conway's Game of Life
The Game of Life is a cellular automaton devised by the British mathematician John Horton Conway, where cells in a grid are either alive or dead, and their state changes based on simple rules.
The main decision variables are the cells in the grid, represented by the 3D array `x`, where `x[k,i,j]` is 1 if the cell at position `(i,j)` is alive at time step `k`, and 0 if it is dead. The `start` array represents the initial state of the grid.
The key constraints are:
- A cell with fewer than two live neighbors dies (loneliness).
- A cell with more than three live neighbors dies (overcrowding).
- A cell with two or three live neighbors stays alive.
- A dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes alive.
These constraints are implemented in the `life` predicate, which calculates the number of live neighbors for each cell and applies the rules to determine the next state of the cell.
The objective of the problem is not to minimize or maximize a specific value, but rather to satisfy the constraints and simulate the evolution of the Game of Life for a given number of time steps `m`, starting from an initial configuration `start`. The solution is the sequence of states of the grid over time, which is output in a human-readable format.
