Pythagorean Triple Problem
The problem is about finding a set of three positive integers that satisfy the Pythagorean theorem, which states that the square of the length of the hypotenuse of a right triangle is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of the other two sides.

The main decision variables are A, B, and C, which represent the lengths of the two shorter sides and the hypotenuse of a right triangle, respectively. These variables are integers, and their values are constrained to be greater than or equal to 1.

The key constraints are that the sum of the squares of A and B must be equal to the square of C, and that A, B, and C must be distinct and in increasing order. In plain language, this means that the triangle must be a valid right triangle with integer side lengths, and that the sides must be in the correct order.

The problem's objective is to find a solution that satisfies all the constraints, rather than to minimize or maximize a specific value.
