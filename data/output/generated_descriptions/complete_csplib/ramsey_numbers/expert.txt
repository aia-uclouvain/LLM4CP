name: Ramsey Problem
description: The Ramsey problem is a classic problem in graph theory and combinatorics that deals with the coloring of edges in a complete graph. The goal is to find the minimum number of vertices in a complete graph such that, when the edges are colored with two colors (typically red and blue), there exists either a monochromatic subgraph of a certain size (k) with all edges of the same color or a monochromatic subgraph of another size (l) with all edges of the same color. In simpler terms, it's about finding the smallest party size where, if guests either know or don't know each other (represented by the colors), there will always be a group of k people who all know each other or a group of l people who all don't know each other.

variables: $x_{i,j}$ for $i < j$, where $x_{i,j}$ represents the color of the edge between vertices $i$ and $j$ in the graph. These variables determine how the edges of the graph are colored, with the domain of $x_{i,j}$ being a set of possible colors (in the given models, the colors are represented as integers, but typically, they would be red and blue).

constraints: $\forall i, j, k \in V, NValues(x_{i,j}, x_{i,k}, x_{j,k}) > 1$ and symmetry constraints. These constraints ensure that there are no monochromatic triangles in the graph (meaning, for any three vertices, not all edges between them have the same color), and the symmetry constraints ensure the graph is undirected (the color of the edge from $i$ to $j$ is the same as from $j$ to $i$).

objective: Minimize $Maximum(x_{i,j})$, which means minimizing the maximum color value used in the graph. This objective is related to finding the smallest possible graph (in terms of the number of different colors needed for its edges) that satisfies the Ramsey problem conditions for given $k$ and $l$.
