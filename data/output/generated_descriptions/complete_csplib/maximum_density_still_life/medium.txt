**Maximum Density Still Life Problem**

The Maximum Density Still Life problem is a classic problem in the field of constraint programming. It is related to Conway's Game of Life, a cellular automaton where cells are born, live, and die based on simple rules.

**Description**

The problem involves finding a still-life pattern with the maximum number of live cells on a grid of size n x n. A still-life pattern is a configuration of live and dead cells that does not change over time. The rules for the Game of Life are as follows:

- A live cell with two or three live neighbors stays alive.
- A live cell with fewer than two live neighbors dies (underpopulation).
- A live cell with more than three live neighbors dies (overpopulation).
- A dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes alive (reproduction).

**Decision Variables**

The main decision variables in this problem are the cells on the grid, which can be either alive (1) or dead (0). The grid is typically surrounded by a border of dead cells to simplify the constraints.

**Constraints**

The constraints in this problem can be described in plain language as follows:

- All border cells are dead.
- Each live cell must have two or three live neighbors.
- Each dead cell must not have exactly three live neighbors.
- The pattern must be still-life, meaning that it does not change over time.

**Objective**

The objective of the problem is to maximize the number of live cells on the grid, while satisfying the constraints. This means finding the still-life pattern with the highest density of live cells.
